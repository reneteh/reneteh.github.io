Posture Protractor

Data-Driven Design, Multi-Sensory Design — 2025

This project began as an exploration of posture awareness, focusing on how poor posture is often overlooked despite its long-term impact on the body. Rather than approaching it as a purely functional correction tool, the process started by observing everyday habits—how people sit, lean, and gradually shift into positions of discomfort without realising it. This revealed a key insight: posture is not just a physical issue, but a behavioural one shaped by habit and lack of immediate feedback. The project therefore aimed to make posture visible and measurable in a way that prompts awareness rather than enforcement.

Through iterative prototyping, the concept evolved into a system that translates body movement into quantifiable data. Early experiments explored ways of tracking body angles and positioning, leading to the development of a physical device that measures posture through angular deviation—similar to how a protractor reads angles. This process involved testing different forms, calibrations, and feedback mechanisms to ensure that the data was both accurate and meaningful. Rather than overwhelming users with complex information, the system was refined to present posture in a simple, legible way, allowing users to immediately understand their body positioning in real time.

The final outcome, Posture Protractor, is a tool that bridges physical movement and data visualisation, transforming posture into something that can be seen, interpreted, and reflected upon. By making subtle shifts in posture visible, the project encourages users to become more conscious of their habits over time. Instead of forcing correction, it creates a moment of self-awareness—where users recognise their own patterns and make adjustments intuitively. Ultimately, the project reframes posture from a passive condition into an active, observable behaviour, highlighting how small, continuous awareness can lead to meaningful change.
