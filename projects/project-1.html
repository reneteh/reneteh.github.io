The Order of Collective Good

Branding, Advertising — 2026

This project began from an investigation into ethical comfort drift, the idea that harmful actions are not immediately accepted, but gradually become normalised through repeated justification. Rather than focusing on what is right or wrong, the process shifted towards understanding how people reason their decisions within structured systems. Early explorations revealed that when actions are framed using language like responsibility, care, and collective good, individuals are more likely to justify discomfort as something necessary. This led to the key question driving the project: how do systems subtly guide people to accept and reinforce ideas they might otherwise resist?



Through iterative development, the project evolved into a constructed belief system titled The Order of Collective Good.

Instead of designing a top-down system, the process focused on participation as the core mechanism. Players are placed in a card-based decision game where they must respond to escalating moral dilemmas using justification prompts. Each decision is recorded into a growing archive known as the “Book of the Collective,” transforming individual reasoning into shared doctrine over time. This structure revealed that belief is not imposed, but built collectively, where repeated justification, validation, and documentation reinforce a system that participants themselves create and sustain.

The final outcome presents a participatory experience that exposes how easily ethical boundaries can shift. As players progress through increasingly uncomfortable scenarios, their justifications remain consistent, rational, and defensible, despite the escalation. The accumulation of these decisions ultimately becomes the most critical moment of reflection, where participants realise that the ideology of the system was not given to them, but constructed through their own actions. By translating this mechanism into an interactive format, the project shifts from simply communicating a concept to allowing users to experience it firsthand, revealing how systems of logic, structure, and collective reasoning can make harm feel acceptable.
