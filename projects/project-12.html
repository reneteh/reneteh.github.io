3D Type - Q

Architectural Typography, Visual Identity — 2023

This project began with an in-depth exploration of typography, focusing on how letterforms are constructed through shape, proportion, and visual balance. Early studies involved analysing type anatomy, kerning, and glyph structures, alongside hands-on exercises such as black-lettering and chalk lettering to better understand the nuances of curves, stroke weight, and spacing . These foundational exercises shifted the approach from simply drawing letters to understanding how they function as structured systems, where even small adjustments in form can significantly affect readability and visual impact.


The process then developed into designing a custom letterform “Q”, drawing inspiration from architectural references, specifically The Hive at NTU. Observations of its layered curves, repetitive structures, and contrasting geometries informed the direction of the form. Through sketching and material experimentation, the letterform evolved from initial concepts into more refined iterations, balancing san-serif clarity with slab-serif characteristics. The challenge was to translate architectural qualities into a legible typographic form while maintaining consistency, rhythm, and proportion. Iterations focused on refining curvature, managing negative space, and ensuring the form retained both structural integrity and visual identity.

The final outcome materialised as a three-dimensional letterform, constructed through foam carving, sanding, and layering techniques. This physical translation required precision and patience, involving multiple stages of shaping, smoothing, and surface finishing using putty and paint to achieve a cohesive form. The addition of subtle protrusions and controlled displacement enhanced the architectural quality of the piece without disrupting its readability. Ultimately, the project bridges typography and spatial design, demonstrating how letterforms can move beyond two-dimensional representation into tactile, sculptural objects shaped by both conceptual thinking and material exploration.
