Father of Brutalism, Le Corbusier

Visual Identity, UIUX — 2024

This project began with an exploration of Le Corbusier and Brutalist architecture, where I intentionally stepped out of my usual graphic design focus to engage with architecture as both content and visual language. Early stages focused on understanding publication fundamentals, grid systems, hierarchy, and binding, while simultaneously developing a visual direction rooted in brutalism’s principles of structure, functionality, and raw expression. Through moodboarding and experimentation, I established a typographic and layout system that was griddy yet intentionally irregular, supported by monochromatic imagery and clean sans-serif typefaces. Material exploration became equally important, where ideas such as translucent overlays, cut-out windows, and layered compositions were used to reflect architectural thinking within the publication itself.

As the project progressed, the publication evolved through multiple iterations of testing and refinement. I experimented with translucent paper overlays to mimic architectural blueprints layered over built forms, as seen in the spreads on page 19, and incorporated physical interventions such as cut-outs that reveal hidden content across pages. The final printed outcome was further elevated through materiality, using black-on-black printing for subtle contrast and pairing the zine with a handcrafted acrylic and concrete-inspired paperweight. This combination extended the brutalist language beyond the page into a tactile object, reinforcing the theme of weight, structure, and physical presence. The process involved continuous problem-solving, from alignment issues during test prints to refining layout, typography, and fabrication decisions through consultation feedback.


Building on the printed publication, the project expanded into a Epub and a motion-based video teaser, translating the same design language across different mediums. The ePub introduced interactivity through buttons, hover states, and animated transitions, allowing users to engage with content in a more dynamic way while maintaining consistency in layout and visual identity. Although technically challenging, particularly in achieving smooth animations and interactive behaviours, it became a key extension of the project’s experiential aspect. The final video further translated the work into motion, using rhythmic editing, typewriter-style text, and structured compositions to mirror the gridded aesthetic of the publication. Together, these outcomes demonstrate a cohesive multi-platform approach, where print, digital, and motion design work collectively to communicate Le Corbusier’s architectural philosophy through both form and experience.
